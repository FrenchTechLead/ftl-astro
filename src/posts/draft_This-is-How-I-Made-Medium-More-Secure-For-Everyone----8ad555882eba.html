This is How I Made Medium More Secure For Everyone 🔒

In late 2020, I reported a serious authentication vulnerability to Medium security staff, Medium has deemed it as a Severity 4 finding and made a new feature to enhance the security of the platform, in this article I will explain the vulnerability and the correction made by Medium.

I consider myself as a cyber-security expert neither a white-hat, but as a Software Engineer, I always make security my first concern when it comes to creating any kind of software, don’t get me wrong I studied some cyber-security at college and I kinda know how to protect from at least the Top 10 Web Application Security Risks, but the main resource that I rely on when I create apps is common-sense.

Thinking like an attacker

Many junior developers do not have yet the ability to see the big picture when developing a feature, they often just get the job done by developing a feature without considering the system in which it’s operating, this misknowledge often results in some weird production bugs or even worst some security vulnerabilities.

Experienced Software Engineers have the ability to flip back and forth between both ways of thinking, playing a kind of mental chess game between opposing forces (Developers ⚙️ VS Hackers 🦹‍♂️).

Ok, so What’s going on with Medium?

Medium has a nice authentication functionality that makes it possible to login to their website without entering any password, actually Medium doesn’t store any user’s password in their database 💡, instead they offer a large choice of social authentication providers and email-based Sign In.

When choosing email Sign in, you receive a temporary private code in your email inbox that lets you access your Medium account, personally, I find this feature interesting because Medium doesn’t have to care about storing, securing, and managing the lifecycle of passwords but while digging deeper in this functionality I made an interesting finding.

When you choose the email-based auth method and provide a non-existing email address, Medium’s server will implicitly respond telling you that the email doesn’t exist in their database.
